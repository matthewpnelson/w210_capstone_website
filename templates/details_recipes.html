Technical Discussion | Recipe Generation

Data

Our recipe database contains over 72,000 different recipes. These recipes are the backbone of the recipe generation models and are critical to accurately training, predicting, and generating new beers from scratch. 

Cleaning 

Although most of the recipes are in the BeerXML standard, there was a significant amount of cleaning that needed to take place in order to use them effectively. Standardization of units, time scales, and nomenclature was an incredible challenge to overcome.

Any brewer will tell you that timing is a critical component to the brewing process. That same brewer will also tell you that recipes list their time information in a way that is counter-intuitive for a novice or computer. While most ingredients are added in a time step relative to the start of the recipe, boil additions are measured from the end of the boil. These time steps all needed ot be standardized into a consistent timeline. You can view this standardized timeline in the Recipe Explorer. This timeline assumes instantaneous temperature changes, which is simply an abstraction for our models, as it is impossible to know how long an individual's stove takes to heat wort.

There is an infinite amount of ingredients that somebody could add into a beer. We decided to narrow it down to a reasonable set of the most commonly used ingredients and not worry too much about Ranch Dressing, rocks, and other unlikely additions. This required a significant amount of work to consolidate all of the possible ways somebody could say "Pale 2-row". Through an advanced aliasing and cleaning system, we managed to capture about 90% of the total ingredients our recipes saw. Unmatched ingredients were placed into bins so that they could still be modeled as unknowns/wildcards in their respective ingredient types.

Scoring Model 

A recipe is a series of steps over time. Our data cleaning and reorganization put these steps into a consistent time-series format. It seemed only natural to select a Recurrent Neural Network as our model for this data. This performed well, but was ultimately rather slow and a little cumbersome. We later opted to use a simpler network architecture as it trained quicker and offered the same performance.

Genetic Algorithm 

Once the Scoring Model was built and tested, the quickest approach to generating new recipes was to simply throw things at the model and see what sticks. We could obviously do better than just randomly generating noise though. Instead, our Genetic Algorithm (GA) randomly selects a few existing recipes, spawns children recipes which are similar to their parents but have been modified slightly, and then allows the best recipes to continue to the next iteration. After many iterations, the great great great grandchild recipe may be very different from its ancestor but will score better against our model and tastebuds.
